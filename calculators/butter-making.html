How It Works

Our butter making calculator estimates yield based on cream fat content and typical butterfat recovery rates. Higher fat cream produces more butter, and different churning methods affect both time and yield efficiency.

Butter Yield Basics

Butter is essentially milk fat that has been separated from cream through churning. The remaining liquid is buttermilk. A typical batch of heavy cream (35% fat) yields roughly 35–40% of its volume as butter, with the rest being buttermilk.

Formula

Cream Weight (lbs) = Cream Volume × Weight per Unit Butter Yield (lbs) = Cream Weight × (Fat% / 100) × Recovery Factor Buttermilk = Cream Volume − Butter Volume Salt = Butter Yield × 0.01 (1% salted butter) Butter Sticks = Butter Yield ÷ 0.25 (¼ lb per stick)
Pro Tip: For best results, let cream warm to 60–65°F (15–18°C) before churning. Cold cream takes longer to churn and may produce lower yield. Always save the buttermilk — it's perfect for baking, pancakes, and salad dressings!

Quick Reference

Typical Butter Yields (per quart of cream)

Cream Type Butter Yield
Light Cream (30%) ~0.6 lb
Heavy Cream (35%) ~0.7 lb
Double Cream (40%) ~0.8 lb
Butter Sticks per lb 4 sticks

One US quart of heavy cream weighs approximately 2.1 pounds and yields about 0.7 pounds (or 2.8 sticks) of butter.

Did You Know?

It takes about 21 pounds (2.5 gallons) of whole milk to produce just 1 pound of butter. That's why butter was historically considered a luxury food item in many cultures.
