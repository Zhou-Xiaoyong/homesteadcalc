🍎 Vegetable Storage Chart

← Back to All Printables

Store the harvest right and eat from the garden all winter. This chart covers ideal conditions and shelf life for every major storage crop, plus what to do without a root cellar.

1. Cold & Moist (32–40°F, 90–95% Humidity)

CropStorage LifePrep & Tips
Carrots4–6 monthsTrim tops to ½"; layer in damp sand or sawdust
Beets3–5 monthsSame as carrots; don't wash before storing
Parsnips4–6 monthsFlavor improves after frost; store like carrots
Turnips / rutabagas4–6 monthsWax rutabagas or keep in damp sand
Cabbage3–4 monthsLeave outer leaves on; wrap heads in paper, store apart (odor)
Potatoes4–8 monthsCure 1–2 wks at 50–60°F first; store DARK at 38–40°F
Apples2–6 monthsWrap individually; store away from vegetables (ethylene!)
Leeks2–3 monthsReplant upright in a bucket of damp sand
Celery / celeriac2–4 monthsRoots in damp sand; keep tops trimmed

2. Cool & Dry (50–60°F, 60–70% Humidity)

CropStorage LifePrep & Tips
Onions5–8 monthsCure 2–3 weeks until necks are papery; braid or use mesh bags
Garlic6–7 monthsCure 3–4 weeks; softneck stores longer than hardneck
Shallots6–8 monthsCure like onions; excellent keeper
Winter squash (butternut)4–6 monthsCure 10 days at 80–85°F; leave 2–3" of stem
Winter squash (acorn)1–2 monthsDo NOT cure acorn types — shortens their life
Pumpkins2–4 monthsCure 10 days; store on boards, not concrete
Sweet potatoes4–7 monthsCure 1–2 wks at 80–85°F/high humidity; store at 55–60°F, never below 50°F
Dry beans / grains1+ yearFully dry, airtight jars, add oxygen absorbers for long term

3. Quick Rules That Save Harvests

🍎 Ethylene Alert

Apples, pears, and tomatoes release ethylene gas that sprouts potatoes and bitters carrots. Store fruit and vegetables in separate areas.

🥕 Don't Wash Roots

Brush off soil but never wash root crops before storage — washing removes the protective skin layer and invites rot.

🔍 Check Weekly

One rotting vegetable spreads fast. Inspect weekly and use blemished produce first — "one bad apple" is literal.

🧅 Onions ≠ Potatoes

Never store onions and potatoes together: onions absorb moisture and rot, and both spoil each other faster.
