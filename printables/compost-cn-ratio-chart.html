♻️ Compost C:N Ratio Chart

← Back to All Printables

Balanced compost comes down to one number: the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio. Aim for 25–30:1 overall. This chart gives you the C:N value of every common material plus recipes that just work.

1. Browns (Carbon-Rich Materials)

MaterialC:N RatioNotes
Wood chips400:1Very slow — best as pathway mulch or in small amounts
Sawdust (aged)300–325:1Use thin layers only; avoid treated lumber
Cardboard (shredded)350:1Remove tape; wet it before adding
Newspaper (shredded)170:1Soy inks are fine; skip glossy pages
Pine needles80:1Break down slowly; fine in moderation
Straw75:1Classic brown — fluffy structure adds airflow
Corn stalks75:1Chop or shred for faster breakdown
Dried leaves60:1The best free brown — stockpile in fall
Hay25:1**Borderline — fresh hay acts closer to a green

2. Greens (Nitrogen-Rich Materials)

MaterialC:N RatioNotes
Horse manure25:1Often mixed with bedding; excellent activator
Cow manure20:1Compost 4–6 months before garden use
Grass clippings20:1Thin layers only — mats and goes anaerobic in clumps
Coffee grounds20:1A "green" despite the color; filters compost too
Fruit & veggie scraps15–20:1Bury in the pile center to deter pests
Alfalfa hay/pellets12:1Powerful nitrogen boost for a stalled pile
Chicken manure7–10:1Hottest manure — compost 6+ months, never use fresh
Blood meal3:1Sprinkle sparingly as a pile activator

3. Hitting the Sweet Spot (25–30:1)

Simple Recipe (by volume)Approx. Blended C:NResult
2 parts dried leaves + 1 part grass clippings~30:1Reliable hot pile
3 parts straw + 1 part chicken manure~28:1Fast, hot, farm-classic mix
2 parts leaves + 1 part kitchen scraps~32:1Steady kitchen composting
1 part sawdust + 4 parts grass clippings~35:1Works, but turn often

👄 Smells Like Ammonia?

Too much nitrogen. Add browns (leaves, straw, shredded cardboard) and turn the pile.

🐌 Pile Not Heating?

Too much carbon or too dry. Add greens (grass, manure, coffee grounds) and water to "wrung-out sponge" moisture.

💧 Moisture Check

Squeeze a handful: a few drops = perfect. Dripping = add browns and turn. Crumbly dry = water while turning.

🌡️ Hot Composting

131–160°F for 15+ days (turning 5 times) kills most weed seeds and pathogens. Use a compost thermometer to verify.

4. What to Keep Out

AvoidWhy
Meat, dairy, oilsOdors, rodents, slow anaerobic breakdown
Dog / cat wastePathogens that survive home compost temperatures
Diseased plantsOnly safe in verified hot piles (131°F+); otherwise trash them
Black walnut leaves/twigsJuglone is toxic to tomatoes and many garden plants
Glossy/coated paperPlastic coatings and questionable inks
Herbicide-treated grassPersistent herbicides (e.g., aminopyralid) can ruin compost for years
